Recovering from Surgery — Bracing & Cold Therapy

The first 6–12 weeks after orthopaedic surgery shape how well you'll recover long-term. The right post-op support — bracing, cold therapy, and motion control trusted by Indian orthopaedic surgeons — reduces pain, prevents setbacks, and accelerates your return to normal life.

What to expect

What does post-surgery recovery actually look like?

Recovery from orthopaedic surgery is a structured 6–12 week process built on three pillars:

1. Pain & swelling control — powered cold therapy and active compression in the first 1–2 weeks, supplemented by medication. Better pain control means earlier movement, which means better long-term outcomes.

2. Structural protection — bracing keeps the surgical repair (graft, fusion, hardware, joint replacement) within safe motion limits while it heals.

3. Gradual progression — modern hinged braces let your surgeon control exactly how much your joint moves week by week. As healing advances, the brace allows more motion until you no longer need it.

Recovery is not linear — there are good days and bad days, and that's normal. What matters is the overall trajectory: less pain week-on-week, more motion, more confident walking. If the trajectory reverses for more than a few days, contact your surgical team.

Surgeries we support

Common orthopaedic surgeries that need post-op equipment

Almost every orthopaedic procedure benefits from some combination of bracing and cold therapy. The most common:

  • Knee surgery — total knee replacement, partial knee replacement, ACL reconstruction, meniscus repair, patellofemoral procedures.
  • Hip surgery — total hip replacement, hip arthroscopy, labral repair.
  • Shoulder surgery — rotator cuff repair, shoulder replacement, dislocation stabilisation, labral repair.
  • Spine surgery — discectomy, fusion, endoscopic decompression, kyphoplasty.
  • Foot & ankle surgery — Achilles repair, ankle fracture, fusion, bunion correction.
  • Hand & wrist surgery — fracture fixation, ligament repair, carpal tunnel release.
  • Sports injury repairs — combining bracing with cold therapy gives the fastest return-to-activity.
The recovery timeline

Your recovery — week by week

Exact timelines vary by procedure, but most orthopaedic recoveries follow this rhythm:

  • Days 1–7 (hospital + early home) — Pain and swelling are at their peak. Cold therapy 4–6 times daily. Rigid bracing or immobilisation continuous. Pain medication on a schedule.
  • Weeks 2–6 (protected motion) — Cold therapy weans down to 2–3× daily. Hinged bracing allows limited motion under surgeon's protocol. Gentle physiotherapy begins. Wound healing should be steady.
  • Weeks 6–12 (active rehab) — Range of motion expands significantly. Functional bracing during specific activities. Strengthening intensifies. Many patients return to desk work in this window.
  • Months 3–6 (return to activity) — Most patients resume normal daily life. Sport-specific bracing only for athletes returning to high-impact activities.

Always follow your surgeon's specific protocol — these phases are general guidance, not medical instruction. Knee replacement, ACL repair, and spine fusion all follow different exact timelines.

The equipment you'll use

Your recovery essentials

Most recoveries use some combination of these. Your surgeon will prescribe which apply to your specific procedure:

  1. Powered cold therapy unit — for the first 1–2 weeks. Most evidence-supported tool for reducing post-op pain medication use.
  2. Rigid post-op brace — for the structural protection phase. Knee, shoulder, hip, or spine, depending on procedure.
  3. Hinged range-of-motion brace — for progressive motion as healing advances. Surgeon-set limits.
  4. Sling or immobiliser — for shoulder and upper-limb surgeries. Worn 24/7 early on.
  5. Walking boot or CAM walker — for foot and ankle procedures.
  6. Functional brace — for the return-to-activity phase. Lighter, less restrictive.
  7. Compression garment — sometimes prescribed for ongoing oedema control.

If you're not sure exactly which your surgeon has prescribed, our team can read the prescription and help you get the right product, sized correctly, in time for your surgery date.

Red flags

When to call your surgeon immediately

Most post-op recoveries proceed smoothly. But these signs are not normal — contact your surgical team or A&E right away:

  • Fever above 38°C (100.4°F) — possible post-surgical infection.
  • Calf pain, swelling, or redness in the operated leg — possible deep vein thrombosis (DVT). Same-day call.
  • Sudden shortness of breath or chest pain — possible pulmonary embolism. Medical emergency — go to A&E.
  • Pus-like or foul-smelling wound drainage — possible deep infection.
  • Pain that suddenly worsens well after the early days — not normal at week 3 or 4.
  • Spreading numbness or tingling — possible nerve compression by the brace or by swelling. Loosen the brace immediately and call.
  • Inability to bear weight when you previously could — possible fracture or graft failure.
Your operating surgeon is the right person for these calls — they know your exact procedure. If you can't reach them, your hospital's emergency line or A&E should be your next step. Avana's clinical team supports the equipment side; we don't replace your surgical follow-up.

Most rigid post-op braces should be removed for showering then replaced immediately. Some early-stage protocols (especially first 2 weeks after shoulder or knee surgery) require continuous wear — your surgeon will tell you. Cold therapy units should never be submerged; wipe the pad clean instead.

Driving timelines vary: typically 2–4 weeks for left-side knee/hip surgery (with an automatic), 4–6 weeks for right-side (where you brake), sooner for arm or shoulder surgery if your driving foot is unaffected. Critically: you must be completely off opioid medication and have enough strength for emergency braking. Confirm with your surgeon before driving.

Procedure-specific. After knee surgery, sleep on your back with a pillow under the heel (NOT under the knee — that can cause knee stiffness). After hip surgery, sleep on your back with a pillow between your knees for the first 6 weeks. After shoulder surgery, often a recliner is easier than a bed. After spine surgery, lie flat or on the side with a pillow between knees. Confirm with your surgeon.
