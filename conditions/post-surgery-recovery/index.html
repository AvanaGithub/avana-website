Post-Surgery Bracing & Cold Therapy

The first 12 weeks after surgery shape your long-term recovery. The right combination of cold therapy, bracing, and surgeon-guided progression — trusted by orthopaedic surgeons across India — can reduce pain, prevent setbacks, and help you return to normal life faster.

Recovering from orthopaedic surgery is as much about what you do after the operation as the operation itself. Post-surgery bracing, powered cold therapy, and surgeon-guided motion control are the three pillars of modern recovery — used widely in Indian hospitals after knee replacement, ACL repair, shoulder surgery, spine fusion, and hip procedures. This page explains how each pillar works, what to expect through your recovery weeks, and which Avana-distributed solutions Indian surgeons most commonly prescribe.

About this stage of care

What does post-surgery recovery actually mean?

Post-operative recovery is the structured 6–12 week process by which a surgically-repaired joint, ligament, or bone heals and returns to function. It has three core components:

1. Pain & swelling control — usually with powered cold therapy and active compression in the first two weeks, supplemented by medication. Better pain control means earlier movement, which means better long-term recovery.

2. Structural protection — bracing or immobilisation that keeps the surgical repair within safe limits while healing happens. ACL grafts, rotator cuff repairs, and joint replacements are all fragile in the early weeks.

3. Gradual progression — modern hinged bracing lets your surgeon dial in exactly how much your joint should move week by week. As healing progresses, the brace lets you do more — until you don't need it.

Common conditions

Who this is for

Surgeries that need post-op bracing or cold therapy

Almost every orthopaedic procedure benefits from some form of post-op support. Most commonly:

  • Knee surgery — total or partial knee replacement, ACL/PCL reconstruction, meniscus repair, patellofemoral surgery.
  • Shoulder surgery — rotator cuff repair, shoulder replacement, dislocation stabilisation, labral repair.
  • Hip surgery — total hip replacement, hip arthroscopy, labral repair.
  • Spine surgery — discectomy, fusion, endoscopic decompression, kyphoplasty.
  • Foot & ankle surgery — Achilles repair, ankle fracture, fusion procedures, bunion correction.
  • Hand & wrist surgery — fracture fixation, ligament repair, carpal tunnel release.
  • Sports injury repairs — generally combine bracing with cold therapy for fastest return-to-activity.
The recovery timeline

Phases of post-surgery recovery

Different equipment matters at different stages. A simplified four-phase view of what most orthopaedic recoveries look like:

  • Phase 1 · Days 1–14 (acute): Pain and swelling are at their peak. Powered cold therapy is critical — used 4–6 times daily. Rigid bracing or immobilisation continuous.
  • Phase 2 · Weeks 2–6 (protected motion): Hinged bracing limits motion to a safe range. Cold therapy weans down. Gentle physiotherapy begins under medical supervision.
  • Phase 3 · Weeks 6–12 (active rehab): Range of motion expands. Functional bracing only during specific activities. Strengthening intensifies.
  • Phase 4 · Months 3–6+ (return to activity): Most patients return to daily life. Sport-specific bracing only for athletes returning to high-impact activities.
What good recovery uses

The three pillars of post-op support

Quality post-surgery recovery layers three categories of equipment, used in sequence:

  1. Powered cold therapy + compression — units like the Polar Care Kodiak and Wave maintain therapeutic cooling for hours and actively pump swelling out of the surgical site. Clinical studies show patients using these need less opioid medication and recover faster.
  2. Rigid post-op bracing — for the first 2–6 weeks, the surgical repair needs to be protected from harmful motion. A correctly-sized rigid brace keeps the joint within safe limits, preventing setbacks that could require re-operation.
  3. Hinged range-of-motion bracing — modern hinges let your surgeon control exactly how much your joint moves at each stage. The brace progressively allows more motion as healing advances.
  4. Functional bracing for return-to-activity — in the later phases, lighter functional braces support the joint during specific activities while the patient regains confidence and strength.
  5. Walking boots and walkers — for foot and ankle procedures, controlled-ankle-motion (CAM) walkers protect the surgical site while letting you walk safely.
  6. Slings and immobilisers — shoulder and upper-limb surgeries need positional support that keeps the joint in a specific safe position 24/7 in early weeks.

Red flags

When to call your surgeon immediately

Most post-op recoveries proceed smoothly. But these signs are not normal — contact your surgical team straightaway:

  • Fever above 38°C (100.4°F) — may indicate post-surgical infection.
  • Calf pain, swelling, or redness in the operated leg — possible deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Sudden shortness of breath or chest pain — possible pulmonary embolism. Medical emergency.
  • Pus-like or foul-smelling wound drainage — possible deep infection.
  • Pain that suddenly worsens well after the early days — not normal at week 3 or 4.
  • Spreading numbness or tingling — possible nerve compression by the brace or by swelling.
  • Inability to bear weight when you previously could — possible fracture or graft failure.
Your operating surgeon is the right person for these calls — they know the exact details of your procedure. Avana's clinical team supports the equipment side; we don't replace your surgical follow-up.
