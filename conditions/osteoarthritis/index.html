About the condition

What is osteoarthritis knee?

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease where cartilage — the smooth tissue covering the ends of bones — gradually thins and wears down. When this happens in the knee, the bones rub against each other, causing pain, stiffness, swelling, and reduced range of motion.

OA most commonly affects the medial (inner) compartment of the knee, especially in Indian adults due to higher rates of squatting, sitting cross-legged, and prolonged standing. Over time, the joint may become bow-legged (varus deformity) as the inner cartilage wears down further.

Unlike rheumatoid arthritis (an autoimmune disease), OA is a "wear-and-tear" condition — it doesn't reverse, but it can be managed and slowed with the right combination of bracing, physiotherapy, weight management, and lifestyle changes. Surgery is one option for advanced cases, but not the inevitable outcome.

Common conditions

What to look for

Symptoms of osteoarthritis knee

Common signs and symptoms include:

  • Pain that worsens with activity — climbing stairs, walking long distances, standing up after sitting.
  • Stiffness on waking that usually loosens up after about half an hour of gentle activity.
  • Swelling and warmth around the knee, especially after activity.
  • A grating or grinding sensation (crepitus) when bending the knee.
  • Reduced range of motion — the knee no longer extends or flexes through its full range without effort.
  • Knee "giving way" during walking or climbing stairs.
  • A bow-legged appearance developing over months or years.
Why it happens

Causes & risk factors

OA knee develops when cartilage in the knee joint wears faster than the body can repair it. Common risk factors:

  • Age — most common after 50, though 35+ is increasingly seen in India.
  • Excess body weight — every extra kilo adds 4 kg of load through the knee while walking.
  • Previous knee injury — ACL tear, meniscus injury, or fracture significantly raises OA risk later in life.
  • Repeated stress on the joint — manual labour, squatting, prolonged kneeling.
  • Genetics — family history of OA is a strong predictor.
  • Female sex after menopause — Indian women have a higher prevalence than men.
  • Bow legs (genu varum) — common in the Indian population, accelerates medial compartment wear.
  • Vitamin D deficiency — prevalent in India, weakens cartilage and bone health.
What helps

Osteoarthritis knee treatment options

OA knee care usually starts with the simplest, least invasive measures — and only moves up to injections or surgery if those don't bring enough relief.

  1. Lifestyle changes — weight management, low-impact exercise (swimming, cycling), avoiding deep squatting, and proper Vitamin D supplementation. Often delivers the biggest pain reduction in early OA.
  2. Physiotherapy — strengthening the quadriceps and core, gait retraining, and manual therapy. Should be paired with bracing for compound effect.
  3. Offloader bracing — the single most evidence-supported non-surgical intervention. An offloader brace applies a corrective force across the knee, shifting load from the worn medial compartment to the healthy lateral compartment. Many patients report meaningful pain reduction and improved walking comfort within weeks of consistent wear.
  4. Pain medication — NSAIDs, topical creams, or short courses of cortisone injections under medical supervision. Manages symptoms but does not slow disease progression.
  5. Hyaluronic acid injections — visco-supplementation that may provide several months of relief in selected patients.
  6. Surgery — arthroscopy, partial or total knee replacement. Reserved for advanced cases where conservative options have failed. Consistent bracing has helped many patients delay knee replacement for years.

Red flags

When to see a doctor

Bracing and lifestyle changes work for most OA knee patients — but some signs need a specialist immediately:

  • Severe pain at rest (not just with activity).
  • The knee locks or gives way repeatedly.
  • Significant swelling that doesn't reduce with rest, ice, or elevation.
  • Inability to fully straighten or bend the knee.
  • Pain that wakes you up at night.
  • Visible deformity developing rapidly.
  • Symptoms in both knees, plus fingers/wrists — may suggest rheumatoid arthritis instead.
